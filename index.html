Non-Local Sensing for Kids: Teacher's Guide

Six-Stage Progressive Training System

Stage I: The Fast Mark

Stage I teaches children to notice and describe their first spontaneous response to a hidden target, before analytical thinking takes over. It's about learning to catch the signal before the mind starts making up stories.

What Stage I Teaches

The Fast Mark

When we hear the target number, something happens in our awareness before we start thinking. A quick gesture, a movement impulse. Stage I teaches the child to let that impulse show up on paper without planning it.

This unplanned mark often contains information about the target's physical character before the conscious mind processes it.

Simple Descriptors

After the mark appears, we describe it with the simplest possible words: how it moved (up, down, across) and how it would feel physically (hard, soft, rough). Not what it might BE, just how it IS.

Keeping descriptions simple avoids analytical guessing. "It went up and feels hard" is data. "It's a mountain" is a guess.

The Helper Word

Sometimes a tiny category word pops up: land, water, building, air. We only take it if it arrives spontaneously. If nothing comes, we write "none."

This teaches distinguishing between what "just appeared" versus what they're thinking about. Signal versus noise.

The Stage I Structure

For each target number, the work appears in this order:

Secret number (e.g., 101)
Fast mark (quick squiggle)
Move: up
Feel: hard
Helper: land

This structure becomes automatic with practice. Eventually the child knows: "I make a mark, I say how it moved and felt, I check for a tiny word, then I'm done."

Running Stage I Sessions

A typical session takes 15-25 minutes and involves 2-4 target numbers, each run 2-4 times.

Before You Begin

Materials

  • Blank paper (one sheet per session)
  • Pencil or pen
  • Target images with assigned numbers (keep hidden)
  • Comfortable, quiet space

Paper Setup

At the top of the page, write:

Name: [child's name]
Place: [e.g., "home," "classroom"]
Date/Time: [today's date and time]

Body Check

Ask: "Is anything in your body or feelings bothering you a lot right now? Are you really tired, or hungry, or do you have a headache, or feel really upset?"

If they mention something but want to continue, write: "Body note: tired" or similar. If very unwell or upset, reschedule.

Introducing Stage I to the Child

"We're going to learn a very quiet skill. I have some secret numbers. Each number is connected to a picture that I'm not showing you yet."

"When I give you a number, you'll write it down (or I'll write it for you). Then your hand will make a fast little squiggle on the paper, all in one go."

"After that, we'll put a few tiny words next to it: one for how it moved, one for how it felt, and maybe one tiny helper word if one pops into your head."

The Stage I Sequence

1
Say the Number

Say: "Write this secret number: 101"

If they can write, let them. If not, write it while they watch.

2
The Fast Mark

Say: "Now put your pen just under the number. Relax your arm and fingers. Let your hand make a fast mark by itself and then stop."

Look for: One quick stroke—curvy, jagged, up, down, across—that appears and is over in a moment.

If they pause: "Don't plan it. Let your hand jump once, quickly, and see what it does."

3
The "Move" Word

Say: "How did your pen move when it made that? Did it go mostly up, mostly down, across, or did it wiggle?"

Examples: up, down, across, curved, wiggly, sharp

Write: "Move: [word]" — Keep it to ONE short description

4
The "Feel" Word

Say: "If you were standing inside that mark, would it feel more hard or more soft?"

Other contrasts: heavy/light, smooth/bumpy, warm/cold

Write: "Feel: [word]"

If they say "feels like a mountain," redirect: "That sounds like what the thing might be. For 'Feel,' let's pick just a body-word like hard, soft, rough, or smooth."

5
The Tiny "Helper" Word

Say: "Sometimes, when you made that fast mark, a tiny word kind of jumps into your head by itself. Not a whole story, just a small word like land, water, building, air, or nothing. Did a tiny word pop up this time?"

Options: land, water, building, air, none

If they hesitate: "If nothing jumps in quickly, we just write 'none' and that's okay."

Repeat the Sequence

After completing one run, say: "We're going to listen to the same number again and see what your hand does this time."

Repeat the entire sequence 2-4 times on the same target number, depending on attention span.

Giving Feedback

After completing your runs on a target number, show the child what that number was pointing to.

Choose Clear Targets

Use targets with strong overall character: open ocean, large mountain ranges, big cities, wide deserts, dense forests.

Point Out Connections

Your job is NOT to judge correct vs. incorrect. Point out meaningful connections.

If "Helper: water" and target is an ocean:

"This little word 'water' fits this place really well, doesn't it?"

If "Move: up" and target has a tall mountain:

"This 'up' fits the tall mountain here."

If a word doesn't match anything visible:

"This one we can't really match to anything in this picture."

Stay neutral. Don't say it's "wrong."

Understanding Breaks

Breaks are named ways to pause when the structure stops working. They teach the child that not forcing an answer is a valid and important response.

Miss Break

When to Use

  • Child says "I don't feel anything" right after the number
  • They stare at the page and can't make a mark
  • They slowly draw a picture instead of making a quick stroke

What to Say

"Let's call that a Miss. That just means we didn't catch the first quick thing this time. I'll write 'Miss' here on the side. Shake your hands out, look around the room, take a breath, and we'll try the same number again."

What to Do

Write "Miss" in the margin. Take a brief pause—shake hands, look around, breathe. Then repeat the number and start fresh.

Why This Works

It's not "you failed." It's "we didn't catch it that time." This keeps the experience light and allows them to try again without anxiety.

Story Break

When to Use

  • Child gives long, detailed stories instead of simple descriptors
  • Example: "It's a big red castle with dragons flying around and flags on top"

What to Say

"That's a story. Stories are fine, but they're different from the little clue words we're practicing. I'm going to write 'Story: big red castle' over here on the side so it has its own place. Then we'll go back to a new fast mark and our little words."

What to Do

Write "Story:" and a brief summary in the margin. Start fresh with the same number: fast mark, Move, Feel, Helper.

Why This Works

It validates stories as real experiences but clearly distinguishes them from descriptive data. The child learns different mental content has different places.

Confused Break

When to Use

  • Child says "It's all mixed up" or "I don't know what I'm doing"
  • You see clear frustration or signs of being overwhelmed

What to Say

"Let's take a Confused break. I'll write 'Confused' here. Let's stand up and stretch, maybe get a drink. Then we can decide if we want to try this number again or save it for another day."

What to Do

Write "Confused" in the margin. Actually stand up and move. After the reset, evaluate: try again, or end this session?

Why This Works

Confusion deserves acknowledgment. Sometimes the signal gets muddy, or they're tired. Naming it and taking a real break often clears things up.

Stage II: Quiet Senses

Stage II adds sensory awareness to the foundation you built in Stage I. The child learns to name simple bodily sensations they would experience at the target location—not what the place is, but what it would feel like to be there.

Critical: Stage II Builds On Stage I

Stage II does NOT replace Stage I. The child still does the complete Stage I sequence (number, fast mark, Move, Feel, Helper), and then adds the new "Senses" block immediately after.

Stage I must be solid before introducing Stage II.

What Stage II Adds

The Senses Block

After completing Stage I, the child adds 3-5 short sensory words: cold, windy, rough, quiet, bright.

This deepens sensory contact with the target without requiring analysis. They're noticing physical qualities, not naming the place.

Contrast Prompts

You guide through specific contrasts: warm or cold? smooth or rough? loud or quiet?

Binary choices prevent getting stuck and keep focus on immediate sensory impressions.

Shaping Away from Labels

When they say "train station," you park it as a story and extract the sensation: "So should we write 'loud' or 'quiet'?"

This teaches the crucial distinction between sensory data and analytical overlay.

The Complete Structure (Stage I + II)

For each target number, the work now appears in this order:

Secret number (e.g., 101)
Fast mark (squiggle)
Move: up
Feel: hard
Helper: land
Senses:
cold
windy
rough
bright

Stage I lines stay exactly the same. You simply add the Senses block underneath.

Running Stage II Sessions

Stage II sessions follow the same structure as Stage I, with the Senses block added after Helper. Sessions still take 20-25 minutes.

Introducing Stage II to the Child

Begin with a clear explanation before using secret numbers.

"Now that you've practiced the number, the fast squiggle, and your first little words, we're going to add a new skill."

"After we finish your Move, Feel, and Helper words, we'll ask: if you were standing at that place, what would your body feel?"

"Not 'what is it,' but things like: cold or warm, rough or smooth, loud or quiet, bright or dark. We'll write down three or four of those body-words."

Model with a Visible Example First

Before using secret numbers, show a simple photograph (beach, forest, city) and demonstrate:

You: "If we were standing here on this beach, what might your body feel?"

Child: "Hot... sandy... windy... loud waves"

You write:

Senses:
hot
sandy
windy
loud

You: "These are the kinds of words we'll add after your fast mark. We won't say 'beach' yet, only the body-words."

The Five Core Prompts

After Stage I is complete, write "Senses:" then lead through these questions. Stop when you have 3-5 sense words.

1. Temperature

"If you were standing at that place, would your body feel more warm or more cold?"

→ Write: "cold" or "warm"

2. Texture

"Does the ground or surface feel more smooth or more rough?"

→ Write: "rough" or "smooth"

3. Moisture

"Does it feel more wet or more dry there?"

→ Write: "wet" or "dry"

4. Sound

"Are your ears hearing more loud or more quiet?"

→ Write: "loud" or "quiet"

5. Light

"Is the place more bright or more dark?"

→ Write: "bright" or "dark"

You don't need all five prompts every time. Keep it light—3-5 words is plenty.

Shaping Away from Labels

Children will naturally offer place-names instead of sensations. Gently reshape this back to body-words.

Example: Story Instead of Sensation

You: "Are your ears hearing more loud or more quiet?"

Child: "It sounds like a train station with lots of people."

You respond: "That sounds like a story about what the place might be. Stories are okay, but they go in a different spot. For 'Senses' we only write body-words like loud or quiet."

Write in margin: Story: train station

Then ask: "So for 'Senses' here, should we write 'loud' or 'quiet'?"

Child: "Loud"

Write under Senses: loud

Example: Full Sentence Instead of Single Word

You: "Would your body feel more warm or more cold?"

Child: "Really cold and windy like on a mountain"

You respond: "That's good. For our list, we'll just write 'cold' right now. The 'windy' we can add next."

Write: cold

Then ask: "Does any wind show up—still or windy?"

Child: "Windy"

Write: windy

Stage II Feedback

When revealing the target, first point out Stage I fits, then shift to Senses.

If they wrote "cold" and picture shows snow:

"This sense word fits this picture really well. Can you see what makes it cold?"

If they wrote "windy" and picture shows waves with whitecaps:

"This 'windy' fits what we can see here—look at these waves."

If you can't tell from the picture:

"This one we can't really match or check from this picture."

You're still not scoring. You're showing that body-words sometimes connect directly with the hidden place.

Session Progression Over Time

Here's how to build skill session by session, from first introduction through mastery.

Stage I Progression (3-6 sessions)

First Session (15-20 min)

  • Explain the concept and demonstrate 1-2 fast marks yourself
  • Run two target numbers
  • For each number, guide through full sequence twice
  • Show pictures and point out clear connections

Second Session (20-25 min)

  • Use three target numbers
  • For each number, aim for 2-3 runs
  • Introduce Miss, Story, and Confused breaks when they occur
  • Let child write numbers and words if ready

Third+ Sessions (20-30 min)

  • Use 3-4 target numbers per session
  • Vary runs per target (2-4) based on engagement
  • Maintain consistent structure every time
  • Child manages most writing; you provide timing and corrections

Stage I is "In Place" When:

  • Child naturally expects: "Number → mark → Move → Feel → Helper"
  • Fast marks appear without hesitation
  • Move/Feel words come easily
  • They understand Helper vs. Story distinction
  • Breaks are used appropriately

Don't add Stage II until this pattern is solid and automatic.

Stage II Progression (3-5 sessions)

First Stage II Session (20-25 min)

  • Explain Stage II and model with visible example
  • Run two secret numbers
  • Guide through full Stage I + all 5 Senses prompts
  • Write sense words for them if needed
  • Point out Stage I fits, then Senses fits

Second Session (20-25 min)

  • Use 2-3 secret numbers
  • Continue contrast prompts but start asking without giving both options
  • If they stall or give labels, fall back to specific prompts
  • Use Story margin consistently

Third+ Sessions (20-30 min)

  • Use 3-4 target numbers
  • Prompt with open instruction: "Give me 3-4 body-words"
  • Only use specific contrasts if needed
  • Child manages most of process

Stage II is "In Place" When:

  • Child expects Senses after Helper without being told
  • Can give 2-3 body-words without every prompt
  • Accepts reshaping from labels without distress
  • Comfortable with all three breaks

Pattern is now stable: Number → Mark → Move → Feel → Helper → Senses

Key Teaching Tips

Keep Sessions Short

20-25 minutes is plenty. Better to end with energy left than push until exhausted.

Consistency Matters

Same header, same sequence, same language. The ritual helps the child relax into the structure.

Stay Neutral

Don't get excited at "hits" or disappointed at misses. Treat all responses as interesting data.

Model Occasionally

Show don't just tell. Demonstrate fast marks or sense words when needed.

Trust the Process

Some sessions feel better than others. The skill develops through repetition, not trying harder.

Celebrate Self-Correction

When they catch themselves starting a story and self-correct to data—that's real learning.

Stage III: Size, Shape, and Simple Sketches

Stage III teaches the child to add basic size and shape information to their Stage I–II data and then to make a simple sketch of the place—a quick "little map" built from the words they've already said.

Stage III Builds On Stages I & II

You never drop Stage I or II. Stage III is added underneath. The child still does the complete Stage I–II sequence, then adds Shapes, whole-place feeling, and sketch.

What You Want by End of Stage III

By the end of Stage III, you want a child who can:

  • Run the full Stage I–II sequence for a target (number, fast mark, Move, Feel, Helper, Senses)
  • Say two or three simple size/shape words (tall/low, wide/narrow, flat/sloping, big/small)
  • Say one short "whole-place feeling" phrase (lonely, busy, peaceful, "don't like it")
  • Use those words to make a simple sketch that shows where the big parts of the place go

The Complete Structure (Stages I-III)

For each target number, the work now appears in this order:

Number
Fast mark
Move: ...
Feel: ...
Helper: ...
Senses: (3-5 words)
Shapes: (2-3 words)
Whole-place feeling: ...
Sketch

Running Stage III Sessions

Stage III sessions add Shape words, whole-place feeling, and a sketch after the Stage II Senses block.

Introducing Stage III to the Child

"Now that you can do the number, fast squiggle, little words, and body-words, we're going to add a new step."

"After your body-words, we'll ask about size and shape: does the place feel tall or low, wide or narrow, flat or sloping, big or small?"

"Then you'll draw a little map of the place using those words. Not a fancy picture—just lines and blobs that show where the big things are."

Demonstrate First

Show a very simple example on scrap paper:

Say: "Shapes: tall, narrow, flat"

Draw: A tall rectangle in the center and a flat area under it

Teaching Shape Words

After finishing the Senses list for a target, write "Shapes:" and lead with clear either/or prompts:

"Does this place feel more tall, or more low?"
"Does it seem more wide, or more narrow?"
"Is it more flat, or more sloping or angled?"
"Does it feel more big, or more small?"

You don't need all four prompts every time. Aim for 2-3 clear answers.

Writing Shape Words

Each answer becomes one short word:

Shapes:
tall
wide
flat

If They Give Longer Sentences

Child says: "It's like a big tall building with lots of levels"

You say: "That's helpful. For Shapes we will only write the most important words. Let's write 'tall' and 'big' this time."

You are teaching that the Shapes line holds short size/shape words only, not detailed descriptions.

Teaching Whole-Place Feeling

Before you sketch, you ask for the child's overall feeling about the place. This is where the child notices how the whole site makes them feel, not just one part.

After the Shapes words, say:

"Now think about the whole place. How does it feel to you inside? Does it feel fun, scary, calm, lonely, busy, peaceful, or something else?"

Encourage a single short phrase, then write it as:

Whole-place feeling: lonely

Whole-place feeling: busy

Whole-place feeling: calm

Whole-place feeling: don't like it

If the feeling is clearly uncomfortable—"scary-no," "I really don't like it"—take note. You can decide to proceed but keep it light, or choose to stop after the sketch and switch to a more neutral target next time. The key is to acknowledge the feeling and give it one line on the page.

Teaching the Sketch

The sketch is built FROM the Stage I–III words, not from a fully imagined picture. You need to tell the child that explicitly.

"Now we'll make a little map of this place. We are not trying to draw a perfect picture."

"We're going to let your shape words turn into lines and blobs. If you said something is tall, we'll draw a tall line or block. If you said flat, we'll draw a flat area. If you said wide, we'll draw something wide."

"We use our words to decide what to draw."

Model It First

It helps to demonstrate once:

Write: "Shapes: tall, narrow, flat"

Draw: A tall narrow block in the center and a flat area under it

Say: "The word 'tall' made me draw this tall block. 'Flat' made me draw this flat bit here. That's all we need."

Guiding the Child to Sketch from Words

Once Shapes and whole-place feeling are written for a secret number, guide the sketch step-by-step:

1
First Shape Word

Point to the first Shapes word and ask:

"You wrote 'tall.' Where does the tall thing belong on your map? More in the middle, to the left, to the right?"

Let them choose, then have them draw a tall vertical line or block there.

2
Second Shape Word

Point to the next Shapes word:

"You wrote 'flat.' Is that flat part under the tall thing, beside it, or all around it?"

They draw a flat area accordingly—like a horizontal rectangle or line.

3
Additional Shapes

Repeat for any additional Shapes words:

"You wrote 'wide.' Is the wide part here or here?"

Encourage them to keep the sketch simple:

"We only need the big pieces. No windows or tiny details—just where the big parts go."

If They Add Details from Mental Images

If they begin adding specific landmarks, people, or decorations, gently redirect:

"That's an interesting detail. Let's first make sure our map shows your 'tall', 'flat', and 'wide' parts. Once that is on the page, you can add a small detail if you still feel like it."

If a strong inner picture is clearly driving the drawing, you can treat it as a "story picture," write "Story picture: ..." in the margin, and bring them back to using their words as instructions.

Practice Pattern for Stage III

For the first few Stage III sessions, keep things light:

  • Use 2-3 secret numbers per session
  • For each number:
    • Run the full Stage I–II sequence
    • Add Shapes words
    • Record a whole-place feeling
    • Guide a quick sketch

Do not worry about how "accurate" the sketches are at first. Your aim is to make the Stage III pattern automatic: Number → fast mark → Move → Feel → Helper → Senses → Shapes → whole-place feeling → sketch.

Feedback with Stage III

When you reveal the target picture, after your Stage I–II feedback you can now point out any useful Stage III connections:

"You wrote 'tall' and drew this tall block. Do we see anything tall in this picture?"

"You wrote 'flat.' Is there a flat area in the picture like in your sketch?"

"Your whole-place feeling word was 'busy.' Does this scene look busy or quiet?"

Sometimes the sketch will match the basic layout nicely. Sometimes parts will match, and sometimes it will not be obvious. You keep feedback gentle and specific, focusing on "This part fits" and "This part we can't really tell from this picture," instead of judging.

Stage IV: Kinds of Place and Soft Meaning

Stage IV lets the child begin to talk about what kind of place the target is and what happens there in very general terms. It uses simple classifications and one or two soft "might" statements.

What You Want by End of Stage IV

By the end of Stage IV, you want a child who can:

  • Run the full Stage I–III sequence
  • Say whether the place feels more like nature or more like buildings
  • Say whether it feels more quiet or more busy
  • Say whether it feels mostly like a play place, work place, learning place, or resting place
  • Write one or two "It might be a place where..." or "It might be a place that feels..." sentences

The Complete Structure (Stages I-IV)

Number
Stage I (Move, Feel, Helper)
Stage II (Senses: 3-5 words)
Stage III (Shapes, whole-place feeling, sketch)
Stage IV:
Kind of place: Nature / Buildings
Quiet / Busy
Mostly: Play / Work / Learn / Rest
"Might" sentences (1-2)

Running Stage IV Sessions

Stage IV sessions add "Kind of place" classifications and "might" sentences after the Stage III sketch.

Introducing Stage IV to the Child

"Now we're going to add a part where we ask what kind of place this feels like. We won't try to name it exactly. We'll use simple choices."

"Does it feel more like nature or more like buildings? More quiet or more busy? More like a place to play, to work, to learn, or to rest?"

"Then we'll write one or two sentences that start with 'It might be a place where...' or 'It might be a place that feels...' because we are using our clues, not saying for sure."

Teaching "Kind of Place" Classifications

After the Stage III sketch, add this section. You can structure it as text lines or choices:

Kind of place:
Nature / Buildings
Quiet / Busy
Mostly: Play / Work / Learn / Rest

For each line, you guide the child to choose one.

The Three Classification Questions

1
Nature vs. Buildings

Ask: "Does this place feel more like nature—like forests, fields, mountains, the sea—or more like buildings and man-made stuff—like cities, roads, factories?"

Let them choose "Nature" or "Buildings." Mark or circle their choice.

2
Quiet vs. Busy

Ask: "Does it feel more quiet, or more busy and full of activity?"

They choose "Quiet" or "Busy" and you note it.

3
Mostly: Play / Work / Learn / Rest

Ask: "Does it feel more like a place where people mostly play, mostly work, mostly learn, or mostly rest?"

Offer examples if unsure:

"Play might be a playground or park. Work might be a farm, factory, or office. Learn might be a school or museum. Rest might be a bedroom, a beach, or a quiet garden."

They choose one: Play, Work, Learn, or Rest

You are helping them turn a vague impression into a few simple qualitative tags without forcing them to name the exact target.

Teaching "Might" Sentences

The last part of Stage IV is to gather one or two short sentences that wrap up their feeling of the place, using the word "might" on purpose.

"Now let's write one or two sentences that start with 'It might be a place where...' or 'It might be a place that feels...'. Remember, we say 'might' because we are just using clues."

Prompting the Sentences

Ask: "Based on your clues, what might people do there?" or "How might it feel to be there?"

Help them turn their answers into short sentences:

"It might be a place where people work with machines."

"It might be a place where kids play outside."

"It might be a place that feels very quiet and peaceful."

"It might be a place that feels busy and loud."

Correcting "IS" to "MIGHT"

If they say "It IS a place where..." gently correct:

"Let's use 'might' here, because we're not saying for sure. Try: 'It might be a place where...'"

You can write the sentences for them if writing is hard; what matters is that they choose the words and hear the soft framing.

Handling Overly Specific Guesses

Children often jump to specific identifications: "It's Disneyland," "It's Grandma's house." Stage IV should acknowledge this without letting the whole output collapse into that guess.

Child says: "It's Disneyland!"

You respond: "That's a strong guess. Let's write that as 'Story: Disneyland' on the side. For our 'might' sentence, use your clues instead. From what you wrote, it feels like a busy play place. How about, 'It might be a place where people go to play outside and it's very busy'?"

You are reinforcing that the structured Stage IV language is about general kind-of-place meaning, while specific guesses are labeled clearly as stories.

Practice Pattern for Stage IV

Introduce Stage IV after the child is comfortable with Stages I–III.

In the first few Stage IV sessions:

  • Use 2-3 secret numbers per session
  • For each number:
    • Run full Stage I–III
    • Add the three "Kind of place" choices
    • Co-construct one or two "might" sentences

As They Gain Confidence

  • Allow them to propose their own "kind of place" words beyond your menu, as long as they stay general ("farm place," "city place," "water place")
  • Gradually shift from heavy prompting to lighter prompting ("Now write an 'It might be...' sentence using your words")

Feedback with Stage IV

When you reveal the target, you can now include Stage IV in your feedback.

"They picked 'Nature'—does this picture show mostly nature or mostly buildings?"

"They picked 'Busy'—does this look like a busy place?"

"They wrote 'It might be a place where people work with machines'—do we see machines here? Does that fit?"

Sometimes their classifications and sentences will fit the main site closely; sometimes they fit the surrounding area; sometimes they do not fit at all. You treat all three with the same neutral commentary:

  • "This fits the place well."
  • "This fits something near the place."
  • "This one we can't really match from the picture."

They keep learning that the job is to follow the structure and express impressions clearly, not to be perfect.

Stage V: One Question at a Time

Stage V introduces "asking the place a question" and listening for one short answer without sliding into a long story. It's a gentle children's version of interrogating the signal: one focused question, one brief answer.

What You Want by End of Stage V

By the end of Stage V, you want a child who can:

  • Run the full Stage I–IV sequence for a target, then
  • Listen for one small answer to a simple question like "What do people mostly do here?" or "What is most important here?"
  • Give that answer in one to three words, stopping before it turns into a story

The Complete Structure (Stages I-V)

Stages I → II → III → IV (as before)
Question: What do people mostly do here?
Answer: grow food

You only ask ONE Stage V question per target with children. That keeps things focused and prevents fatigue.

Good Stage V Questions

Keep questions simple and about general function or mood, not about names:

"What do people mostly do here?"

"What is the main thing that happens here?"

"What is the strongest feeling here?"

"What is the most important thing here?"

Avoid questions like "What is this place called?" or "What country is this in?" which invite guessing rather than sensing.

Running Stage V Sessions

Stage V sessions add one focused question with a short answer after Stage IV.

Introducing Stage V to the Child

"Now we're going to learn one more move. After you do all your clues and sentences, you get to ask the place a tiny question, like 'What do people mostly do here?' or 'What is the most important thing here?'"

"Then we listen quietly and write down just a very short answer. If your brain starts telling a long story, we'll call it a story and park it like before."

The words "tiny question" and "very short answer" are important. They signal the scale of what you want.

Choosing Stage V Questions

As the adult, you choose the Stage V question for each target. Keep questions simple and always about general function or mood, not about names.

Good Examples:

  • "What do people mostly do here?"
  • "What is the main thing that happens here?"
  • "What is the strongest feeling here?"
  • "What is the most important thing here?"

Avoid:

  • "What is this place called?"
  • "What country is this in?"

These invite guessing rather than sensing.

Teaching the Stage V Move

After the Stage IV "might" sentences for a target, pause and say:

"Now we'll do our tiny question. For this one, the question is: 'What do people mostly do here?'"

"Close your eyes for a moment. Think of the place your number is pointing to. Just once, very softly in your mind, ask, 'What do people mostly do here?' Then notice the first small answer that pops up, like a word or tiny phrase. We only want one little answer, not a whole story."

Give them a few seconds in silence.

Then ask: "What little answer popped up?"

Shaping the Stage V Answer

When the child replies, your job is to keep the answer short and to separate short answers from stories.

If They Give a Simple Answer:

Child says: "grow food"

You write:

Question: What do people mostly do here?
Answer: grow food

Other examples: drive cars, pray, swim, rest

If They Give a Sentence:

Child says: "They walk around and buy food from stands"

You say: "That's helpful. Let's capture that as two words: 'buy food'"

You write: Answer: buy food

If They Launch into a Story:

Child says: "It's a big city where people run around and there are shops and cars and lights and my friend is there..."

You respond: "That's turning into a story. Stories are okay; let's park that as 'Story' on the side. For our tiny question, we just pick one small answer, like 'shop' or 'run' or 'busy.' Which word feels most right?"

You write: Story: big city with shops and cars (margin)
Answer: shop

You are teaching that Stage V answers are brief and must stop before they unfold into a long scene.

Practice Pattern for Stage V

Stage V works best if you keep it very light:

  • Don't use Stage V on every target in the beginning. In early sessions, choose 1-2 targets per session to add Stage V to, after the child has done Stage I–IV.
  • Use only ONE question per target.
  • Keep questions consistent for a while (for example, use "What do people mostly do here?" across several sessions) so the child understands the pattern.

A Simple First Stage V Session Might Look Like:

  • Two secret numbers with full Stage I–IV, no Stage V
  • One secret number with full Stage I–IV, plus Stage V question "What do people mostly do here?" and a short answer

Feedback with Stage V

When you reveal the target, Stage V gives you one more small point of comparison. After Stage I–IV feedback, look at the Stage V question and answer:

"Your question was 'What do people mostly do here?' and you answered 'grow food.' Does this picture look like a place where people grow food?"

If it fits, you say so; if it fits something nearby; or if you cannot tell, you say that. You keep the same neutral style:

  • "This fits what we see here."
  • "This could fit something near this place."
  • "This one we can't really check in this picture."

You do not weigh Stage V more heavily than the other stages. It is just one more small piece of data.

Stage VI: Building the Place

Stage VI invites the child to build a simple 3D model of the place using clay, blocks, or cardboard, based on what they've already written and drawn. The hands-on building anchors impressions physically and can bring out additional observations.

What You Want by End of Stage VI

By the end of Stage VI, you want a child who can:

  • Run the full Stage I–V sequence for a target, then
  • Use a few basic materials to build a very rough model of the site (a tall block for a building, a slope for a hill, a flat base for ground or water)
  • Optionally add 1-2 extra words that arise while building, still keeping them short and concrete

When to Use Stage VI

Stage VI is not needed for every target. It is best used occasionally as a capstone for a target that produced a rich Stage I–V session.

You might:

  • Use Stage VI on one target per session, or
  • Reserve it for "special" targets when you want a deeper dive

Stage VI works best when: the child has given a decent set of Stage I–III words, the sketch is reasonably clear, and the child is still engaged and not tired.

Materials Needed

You don't need fancy materials. A simple kit can include:

  • A tray or sheet of cardboard as a base
  • Modeling clay or play-dough
  • A handful of small blocks or boxes
  • Strips of paper or pipe cleaners for rivers, roads, or boundaries
  • Crayons or markers if needed to mark areas

The Complete Structure (Stages I-VI)

Stages I → II → III → IV → V (as before)
Stage VI: Build 3D model from words and sketch
Extra impressions while building: (1-2 optional)

Running Stage VI Sessions

Stage VI sessions add 3D building after Stage V (or Stage IV). This is occasional, not for every target.

Introducing Stage VI to the Child

"Now we're going to try building this place. We're not making a perfect model. We're going to use clay and blocks to show the big shapes from your words and your map."

"If you wrote 'tall,' we'll build a tall thing. If you wrote 'flat,' we'll make a flat part. Your hands will follow your clues."

Disconnect this from the idea of "craft time." It's brief and focused, not an open-ended art project.

Teaching the Building Move

For a chosen target (one that has a Stage III sketch and Stage IV notes you like), set out the materials and the child's paper. Work on a tray or base so you can refer to "left" and "right."

1
Identify the Biggest Shape

Place the paper with the sketch visible.

Say: "Look at your Shapes and your map. What is the biggest shape here?"

They might say "The tall thing in the middle," "The flat ground," or "The wide area on the left."

2
Build the First Piece

Say: "Use a block or clay to make that tall thing. Put it on the tray where it is on your map."

3
Add the Next Biggest Piece

Ask: "What is the next biggest piece?"

They might say "the flat," "the slope," or "the wide part."

Help them build that around the first piece, using clay or paper.

4
Complete Main Elements

Continue until they've represented:

  • Tall vs. low
  • Wide vs. narrow
  • Slopes vs. flat areas
  • Perhaps a strip for a road or river

The model should stay simple. A few shapes are enough.

If They Start Adding Small Details

"Okay, you added a little thing there. Remember our main job is to show the big shapes from your words. Do we still see your 'tall', your 'flat', your 'wide' in this model?"

Allowing Extra Words During Building

Sometimes while they build, the child may say things like:

  • "Now it feels more closed in"
  • "It feels higher than I thought"
  • "I think there's water behind this"

You can choose to capture 1-2 of these as extra notes, as long as you keep them short and do not let them turn into stories.

You might write:

Extra impressions while building:

feels higher

more closed in

water behind

If the comments drift into long imaginative stories, you treat them as Story in the same way as before: "That's a story; let's park it on the side," and bring them back to the main shapes.

How Often to Use Stage VI

Stage VI takes more time and energy than the other stages. You do not need to use it for every target.

You might:

  • Use Stage VI on one target per session, or
  • Reserve it for "special" targets when you want a deeper dive

Stage VI works best when:

  • The child has given a decent set of Stage I–III words
  • The sketch is reasonably clear
  • The child is still engaged and not tired

Feedback with Stage VI

When you finally show the target picture, you can now look at:

  • Stage I–IV notes
  • Stage V answer, if used
  • The Stage III sketch and Stage VI model

You can ask:

"How does your model match this picture?"

"Do we see a tall thing where you built one?"

"Is this flat or sloping like you made it?"

"Does this feel more closed in or open, like you said?"

Again, you highlight what fits, what fits nearby, and what cannot be checked, without turning it into a scored test.
