Seeds and Parts

A Three-Lesson SEL Curriculum for Children Ages 6–9

Teaching children to notice emotions as inner experiences they can observe and care for

Introduction for Adults

What This Curriculum Is

This is a three-lesson social–emotional learning sequence for children ages 6–9, plus an optional creative extension. It teaches children to notice emotions as inner experiences they can observe and care for, rather than as fixed identities.

The curriculum weaves together two sources:

Thich Nhat Hanh's "Seeds" Metaphor

Thich Nhat Hanh, a Buddhist teacher and peace activist, described the mind as a garden containing many seeds: seeds of joy, calm, anger, fear, compassion, and so on. When life events "water" these seeds, they sprout as feelings and impulses in the moment. We cannot remove seeds from the garden, but we can learn which ones to water and which to tend with care.

Internal Family Systems (IFS)

IFS is a psychotherapy model that understands the mind as made up of different "parts"—inner sub-personalities or clusters of feelings that show up in particular situations. Instead of saying "I am angry," a person might say "a part of me is angry." The key idea is that there is also a centered "Self"—a calm, clear, compassionate inner presence—that can listen to and care for any part, even difficult ones. No part is inherently bad; all parts are trying to help in their own way.

For children, this curriculum does not teach therapy. It borrows only the developmentally natural pieces: "I have different sides inside," "none of my parts are bad," and "there is a part of me (Self) that can be kind and listen." These align with standard social–emotional learning goals: identifying emotions, building self-awareness, and developing coping skills.

Why These Two Together

The seeds metaphor gives children a poetic, natural image (a garden) that is easy to remember and draw. The parts language gives them permission to step back from a big feeling and notice it, as something inside them rather than as all of them. Together, they create space:

"I am not my anger; I have an angry part, and I also have a Self that can notice and care for it. What seed am I watering right now?"

What This Is Not

  • This is not therapy or trauma processing.
  • This is not a replacement for trained mental health support when a child needs it.
  • This is not an expectation that children will disclose painful stories or private struggles.
  • This does not assume prior knowledge of Buddhism, IFS, or any other framework on the part of teachers or parents.

What Adults Need to Know

You do not need to be trained in IFS or meditation to teach this. You need:

  • A basic comfort with the language introduced here.
  • The ability to model a calm, curious attitude toward feelings (yours and children's).
  • Clear boundaries: practices are light, optional, and always bounded by "you can pass."
  • Consistency in how you frame these ideas across the three lessons.

Core Concepts in Plain Language

The Seeds

Inside each person is a garden of seeds. These seeds are qualities and emotions: calm, joy, anger, fear, kindness, silliness, worry, courage. When something happens—a friend laughs, we lose a game, we get praise—a seed wakes up and grows into a feeling or impulse in that moment.

We cannot throw seeds away. We cannot have a garden with only "good" seeds. But we can learn to water some seeds more often (calm, courage, kindness) and to tend other seeds gently so they do less harm (anger, fear). Over time, through small choices about what we pay attention to and what we practice, different seeds grow stronger or quieter.

For children: Emotions come and go like seeds sprouting. I can notice them, care for them, and influence what grows.

Parts and Self

Everyone has parts. A part is a cluster of feelings, thoughts, and body sensations that tends to show up in certain situations. For example:

  • "The part of me that worries when I might be wrong."
  • "The part of me that gets angry when things feel unfair."
  • "The part of me that gets silly when I'm nervous."

These parts are not problems. They developed because they were trying to help. The anxious part developed to help you be careful. The angry part developed to help you stand up for yourself. The silly part developed to help you feel less alone.

But sometimes a part takes over, and you feel like that part is all of you. That is where Self comes in.

Self is a centered, calm inner presence that can notice any part without being taken over by it. Self is curious, not judgmental. Self can say to a part: "I see you. I hear that you are trying to help. Thank you. I am here now, and I can take it from here."

When Self is present, parts soften. They relax. They do not have to work so hard.

For children: I have different sides inside me. None of them are bad. There is a calm part of me (Self) that can listen and be kind to all of them.

How They Work Together in This Curriculum

  • Lesson 1 introduces the garden and names that we have parts.
  • Lesson 2 teaches children to listen to one part from Self—to step into that calm, curious space and ask a part questions.
  • Lesson 3 connects caring actions to "watering seeds"—choosing what to practice and pay attention to.
  • The extension lesson gives children a structured way to give voice to a part in poetry or song, deepening understanding without over-identifying.

Lesson 1: I Have Many Seeds and Parts

Adult Aim

Introduce the mind-as-garden metaphor. Normalize that we have many inner characters or "parts" linked to different feelings. Build a basic somatic awareness (where in the body do I feel this?).

Child Learning Targets

  • I can name one feeling I have.
  • I can point to where I feel it in my body.
  • Feelings are like seeds that come and go; they are not who I am.

Lesson Flow (30–35 minutes)

1. Opening: Context and Breath (5 minutes)

Adult says:

"Today we are going to practice noticing our feelings and our bodies so we can take better care of them. This is not about good feelings or bad feelings. It is about being kind gardeners to everything that grows inside us.

"We are going to use a slow breath to help our bodies know they can slow down and rest. You can do this with me, or you can just sit and feel your hands. There is no right way."

Breath with explanation and full choice:

"Breathe in slowly, as if you are gently smelling something sweet. Breathe out slowly, as if you are softly blowing a seed across the air. Let's try it together three times."

(Do this with the group. Keep it simple and unhurried. Offer it as a tool, not a command.)

2. Somatic Check-In (5 minutes)

Adult asks:

"Now that you have slowed down a bit, notice your body. What does it feel like right now? Is it tight or soft? Heavy or bouncy? Warm or cool?"

(Pause. Give time for noticing, not answering. Some children will want to share; some will not.)

"Where do you feel your strongest feeling right now? Is it in your tummy, your chest, your throat, your face, your hands, or somewhere else?"

Activity:

Give each child a simple body outline (head, torso, arms, legs). Ask them to point to or circle the place where they feel something the most. Then ask them to write or tell you one word for what it feels like: tight, hot, wiggly, heavy, calm, buzzy, loose, etc.

(This is not about accuracy. It is about the practice of turning inward and noticing.)

3. Core Teaching: The Mind Garden (8–10 minutes)

Adult tells this story, in own words:

"Inside each of us is a garden. In this garden are many seeds. There are seeds of calm, and seeds of joy. There are seeds of anger, seeds of worry, seeds of silliness, seeds of kindness, seeds of courage.

"When something happens in the day—a friend laughs at you, you win a game, someone is mean—a seed wakes up and grows. That is a feeling.

"Sometimes a seed grows into a little character, like a part of you. We might call it the Shy Part, or the Angry Part, or the Playful Part. These parts are not bad. They are trying to help you in their own way.

"For a long time, you might not have noticed all these different parts. But they are always there. And the more you notice them, the kinder you can be to them, and the more you can choose which seeds to water."

Make it concrete:

Name 3–4 parts that kids experience in daily life. For example:

  • "The part that worries about being wrong."
  • "The part that gets mad about fairness."
  • "The part that gets silly when nervous."
  • "The part that wants to fit in."

4. Activity: Draw Your Feeling Seed or Part (10–15 minutes)

Prompt:

"Think of one feeling you had this week. It can be happy, sad, mad, worried, silly, calm—any feeling. Imagine it as a seed or a little character inside you. Draw it. You can give it a name if you want."

Sentence frames (children can dictate, fill in, or write, depending on age):

  • "This part shows up when…"
  • "In my body it feels like…"

(Keep it open-ended. Some children will draw. Some will want to talk first. Some will write a lot. Honor all of it.)

5. Closing: Introduce Self (3–5 minutes)

Adult says:

"There is also something in you called Self. Self is the you who can notice all your seeds and parts and can be kind to them. Self is never bad, even when a big feeling is here. Self is the gardener.

"When we come back next time, we will practice listening to one part, from Self."

Optional check-out:

"Who wants to share one seed or part you noticed today?" (No pressure; this is invitation only.)

Lesson 2: Self Listens to One Part

Adult Aim

Give children a direct experience of stepping into a calm, curious inner presence (Self) and asking a part questions. Begin with a mild, safe feeling. Keep the role-play or conversation boundaried and light.

Child Learning Targets

  • I can remember that I have parts and that I also have Self.
  • I can ask at least one curious question to a part.
  • A part can tell me something about when it shows up and what it is trying to do.

Lesson Flow (30–35 minutes)

1. Opening: Recap and Breath (5 minutes)

Adult says:

"Last time we learned that we have many seeds and parts inside us, like a garden. Who remembers a part they drew?

"Today we are going to practice listening to one part, from Self. We will use the same slow breath to help our bodies feel safe."

Breath (same as before, but now framed differently):

"Breathe in slowly, as if smelling something gentle. Breathe out slowly. This helps our body know it is safe enough to listen inside."

(Do three breaths together.)

2. Somatic Check-In (5 minutes)

Adult asks:

"Scan your body from head to feet. Where do you feel something the most today? Can you point to that place or just notice it in your mind?"

(Pause for noticing.)

"What does it feel like? Tight? Loose? Hot? Cool? Wiggly?"

(Children name a sensation. Some may also name a feeling; that is a bonus.)

3. Core Teaching: Self and Parts (8–10 minutes)

Adult explains:

"Self is the part of you that can be calm, kind, and curious. It is like the gardener in your mind garden. When you are in Self, you can notice your feelings without being taken over by them.

"A part is a feeling or inner character that might be strong or quiet. Sometimes a part gets loud, and it feels like that is all of you. But Self can say to a part: 'I see you. I hear you. I am listening.'

"When Self listens, parts often relax. They do not have to work so hard."

Write three sample questions on the board or chart:

  • "What are you worried/mad/sad about?"
  • "When do you usually show up?"
  • "What do you wish I knew?"

(Read these aloud. These are the tools children will use.)

4. Role-Play with Clear Options and Boundaries (12–15 minutes)

Step 1: Choose a part

Adult says: "Pick a feeling that is safe to explore here today. Not the scariest one, but something real. If a feeling feels too big, pick a different one."

(Children think of one mild-to-moderate part from their life or from their drawing last lesson.)

Step 2: Choose how the part will speak

"Now, you get to choose how your part will speak. You can use:

  • A doll
  • A puppet
  • Acting it out with a partner
  • Speaking as your drawing
  • Or just telling me in a part voice"

(Offer choices. This gives children agency and makes it feel safe and playful.)

Step 3: Dialogue

In pairs or with the adult:

  • One person is "Self" (calm, curious, kind).
  • One person is "Part" (the feeling or inner character).
  • Self uses at least one of the three posted questions.
  • Part answers in first person: "I show up when..." or "I feel like..."

(The adult models Self if needed. Keep it short—3–5 minutes per pair. Tone is gentle and curious, not clinical.)

Boundaries the adult restates:

"You do not have to talk about secrets. You do not have to share anything private. You can say 'pass' or 'not ready' at any time. This is practice, not therapy."

5. Closing (3–5 minutes)

Reflection prompts (children speak or write):

  • "Something my part told Self was…"
  • "One kind thing Self can say to a part is…"

Final settling:

"Take one slow breath. Put your hand on your heart or your belly. Remember: Self is the you who can listen and be kind to all your parts."

Lesson 3: Caring for a Part, Watering Seeds

Adult Aim

Connect the idea of "watering seeds" to concrete, child-friendly coping strategies. Help children see that what they do, what they think, and what they pay attention to all influence which seeds grow stronger.

Child Learning Targets

  • I can name at least one way to care for a part.
  • I understand that choices (breath, movement, what I do, who I talk to) can help calm or support a part.
  • I can make a personal "care plan" for one part I know.

Lesson Flow (30–35 minutes)

1. Opening: Recap and Purpose (5 minutes)

Adult says:

"We learned that Self can listen to parts. Today we learn what helps a part feel cared for. Remember the garden? When we water seeds of calm, courage, and kindness, those seeds grow stronger. Today we will make a plan for how to water good seeds when a part needs care."

Breath or mindful movement:

"Let's do three slow breaths" or "Let's take three mindful steps in place. Feel your feet on the ground. Each step says, 'I am here.'"

(Offer choice: breathe or step, or just sit and feel.)

2. Somatic Check-In (5 minutes)

Adult asks:

"Notice your body now. Is it more awake, sleepy, tight, soft than before?"

(Pause.)

"Where in your body does a part need care right now? Point to it, or just know it in your mind."

3. Core Teaching: Watering Seeds (8–10 minutes)

Adult explains:

"Remember, in the garden, seeds grow when they are watered. When we speak kindly, breathe slowly, move our bodies, ask for help, or spend time with people we trust, we water seeds of calm, courage, and understanding.

"When we say mean things to ourselves, stay stuck in fights, or spend a lot of time on things that make us more upset, we water seeds of anger and fear.

"Self can choose which seeds to water, even when parts are loud and big."

(Make it concrete with an example from daily life: "If the Worried Part is loud about a test or a challenge, we can water the Calm Seed by talking to a trusted adult, taking three slow breaths, or remembering one time we did okay.")

Group brainstorm:

"Let's think about different parts and what helps them. I will write our ideas on the board."

Ask:

  • "What might help a Worried Part?" (Children answer: slow breaths, talking to an adult, naming 5 things in the room, hugging a toy, remembering something good.)
  • "What might help an Angry Part?" (Children answer: stomping safely outside, squeezing a pillow, drawing the anger, saying, "You are angry because you care about fairness.")
  • "What might help a Sad Part?" (Children answer: talking to someone, listening to soft music, cuddling a toy, drawing, crying.)

(Write all ideas without judgment. They do not have to be "healthy"; they just have to be safe and appropriate for the setting.)

4. Activity: Care Plan Card (12–15 minutes)

Individual Care Plan Card:

Give each child a card or paper divided into three sections:

Front:

  • Picture or name of one part they want to care for.

Back:

  • "This part shows up when…" (one sentence)
  • "In my body it feels like…" (one or two words)
  • "It feels better when I…" (three choices they will try this week)

(Children complete this. They choose one concrete action they are willing to try in real life.)

5. Closing (3–5 minutes)

Guided settling image:

"Close your eyes if that feels okay. Imagine your part in a safe place in your garden. Imagine giving it one of the helpful things from your card. Notice what your body feels like now. Is it a little softer? A little calmer?"

(Pause 30 seconds.)

Final words:

"Self is the gardener who can notice seeds, listen to parts, and choose what to water. You get to make those choices."

Extension Lesson (Optional): A Part Speaks in a Poem or Song

Adult Aim

Offer a bounded creative way to give voice to a part and deepen understanding. Use externalizing language (art, poetry, character voice) so children do not over-identify with a difficult feeling. Always end by returning to Self's response.

Child Learning Targets

  • I can express what a part thinks or feels in my own voice or a part's voice.
  • I can also express how Self responds to that part.

Lesson Flow (25–30 minutes)

1. Safety Frame and Context (3–5 minutes)

Adult says:

"Today we will let one part speak in words or song. We will choose a feeling that is safe to explore here today. If a feeling feels too big or uncomfortable, you can pick a different one, or you can write from a helper part instead.

"We are not trying to get everything out. We are practicing listening and caring."

Somatic check-in and breath (same as before):

"Let's take one slow breath together so our bodies know this is safe."

2. Option A: Part-Voice Poem (10–15 minutes)

Write these sentence starters on the board:

  • "I am the part that shows up when…"
  • "I feel in the body like…"
  • "I try to help you by…"
  • "I feel calmer when you…"

Instructions:

"Using these lines, write or tell me what your part would say. You can dictate to an adult, fill in the frames, or write it yourself, depending on what feels right."

(Children work. Adult scribe if needed.)

After the part stanza, add a Self line:

"Now, what would Self say back to this part?"

Adult writes: "From Self: I hear you. I will try to…"

(Children complete: "I will try to listen better," "I will try to slow down," etc.)

3. Option B: Class Call-and-Response Song (10–15 minutes)

Choose one common part as a group:

"Let's pick one part that many of us have. How about the part that worries about doing well? Or the part that gets mad about fairness?"

(Vote or let children choose.)

Create verses in the part's voice:

"What would this part say? How would it describe itself?"

Example:

  • "Sometimes I twist your tummy when there is a test."
  • "I show up when you think you might get things wrong."

Create a refrain in Self's voice:

"And what would Self say back?"

Example:

  • "I hear you. Let's breathe and remember who helps us."
  • "You care about doing well. That is good. I am here now."

(Sing it together a few times. Keep it simple and somewhat playful, not heavy.)

4. Closing: Re-anchor in Self (5 minutes)

Reflection:

  • "What did you learn about this part?"
  • "What did Self say that felt helpful?"

(Children answer or pass.)

Final grounding:

"Let's take one slow breath. Now notice one thing in your body that feels calm or okay—maybe your hands, your feet, your back against the chair."

(Let them name it: "My feet on the ground." "My hands are warm.")

"That is Self, noticing and caring. Good work today."

Notes for Teachers

Scope and Sequence

Why These Ideas Build Incrementally

  • Lesson 1 introduces the core metaphor (garden/seeds) and the basic concept (I have parts). It does not ask children to do anything with the parts yet—only to notice and name them.
  • Lesson 2 adds the idea of Self and asks children to practice one simple dialogue: asking a part one question. This is the first moment of "stepping back" and relating to a feeling, rather than just having it.
  • Lesson 3 adds the element of action: "If I have a part, what do I do about it?" It connects feeling to concrete strategy and introduces the idea that my choices matter.
  • Extension deepens this with creative expression, only after children have already practiced noticing, listening, and caring.

Differentiation by Age (Within the 6–9 Range)

Ages 6–7:

  • Shorter role-plays (2–3 minutes).
  • More visual supports (lots of drawing, fewer words).
  • Simpler sentence frames; dictation preferred over writing.
  • Stick to mild emotions (happy, silly, calm, worried about small things).

Ages 8–9:

  • Longer dialogues (4–5 minutes).
  • Can write more independently.
  • Can handle slightly more nuanced feelings (embarrassed, frustrated, left out).
  • More time for brainstorm and reflection.

Ongoing Practice Between Lessons

In real moments (not full lessons):

  • When a child is upset, an adult might say: "I notice you have an angry part right now. What is it trying to tell you?" or "What would help that part feel better?"
  • Create a poster: "Questions for parts" (from Lesson 2) or "Care ideas" (from Lesson 3) that you reference regularly.
  • Brief check-ins during transitions or reflective moments: "Which part of you showed up today?" or "What seed did you water today?"

Guardrails for Adults

Important Boundaries

  • Keep it light and playful, not clinical or therapeutic.
  • Do not push a child to share. Offer the invitation; respect the pass.
  • Do not use this framework to process trauma or painful disclosures. If a child shares something serious, listen, thank them, and connect them to a counselor or trusted adult.
  • Model the language yourself so it becomes normalized: "A part of me is tired today" or "My calm part is strong when I take a walk."
  • Always include an opt-out: "You can do this, or you can sit and observe."
