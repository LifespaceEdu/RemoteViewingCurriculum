Remote Viewing for Kids: Teacher's Guide

Stages I & II

Stage I: The Fast Mark

Stage I teaches children to notice and describe their first spontaneous response to a hidden target, before analytical thinking takes over. It's about learning to catch the signal before the mind starts making up stories.

What Stage I Teaches

The Fast Mark

When we hear the target number, something happens in our awareness before we start thinking. A quick gesture, a movement impulse. Stage I teaches the child to let that impulse show up on paper without planning it.

This unplanned mark often contains information about the target's physical character before the conscious mind processes it.

Simple Descriptors

After the mark appears, we describe it with the simplest possible words: how it moved (up, down, across) and how it would feel physically (hard, soft, rough). Not what it might BE, just how it IS.

Keeping descriptions simple avoids analytical guessing. "It went up and feels hard" is data. "It's a mountain" is a guess.

The Helper Word

Sometimes a tiny category word pops up: land, water, building, air. We only take it if it arrives spontaneously. If nothing comes, we write "none."

This teaches distinguishing between what "just appeared" versus what they're thinking about. Signal versus noise.

The Stage I Structure

For each target number, the work appears in this order:

Secret number (e.g., 101)
Fast mark (quick squiggle)
Move: up
Feel: hard
Helper: land

This structure becomes automatic with practice. Eventually the child knows: "I make a mark, I say how it moved and felt, I check for a tiny word, then I'm done."

Running Stage I Sessions

A typical session takes 15-25 minutes and involves 2-4 target numbers, each run 2-4 times.

Before You Begin

Materials

  • Blank paper (one sheet per session)
  • Pencil or pen
  • Target images with assigned numbers (keep hidden)
  • Comfortable, quiet space

Paper Setup

At the top of the page, write:

Name: [child's name]
Place: [e.g., "home," "classroom"]
Date/Time: [today's date and time]

Body Check

Ask: "Is anything in your body or feelings bothering you a lot right now? Are you really tired, or hungry, or do you have a headache, or feel really upset?"

If they mention something but want to continue, write: "Body note: tired" or similar. If very unwell or upset, reschedule.

Introducing Stage I to the Child

"We're going to learn a very quiet skill. I have some secret numbers. Each number is connected to a picture that I'm not showing you yet."

"When I give you a number, you'll write it down (or I'll write it for you). Then your hand will make a fast little squiggle on the paper, all in one go."

"After that, we'll put a few tiny words next to it: one for how it moved, one for how it felt, and maybe one tiny helper word if one pops into your head."

The Stage I Sequence

1
Say the Number

Say: "Write this secret number: 101"

If they can write, let them. If not, write it while they watch.

2
The Fast Mark

Say: "Now put your pen just under the number. Relax your arm and fingers. Let your hand make a fast mark by itself and then stop."

Look for: One quick stroke—curvy, jagged, up, down, across—that appears and is over in a moment.

If they pause: "Don't plan it. Let your hand jump once, quickly, and see what it does."

3
The "Move" Word

Say: "How did your pen move when it made that? Did it go mostly up, mostly down, across, or did it wiggle?"

Examples: up, down, across, curved, wiggly, sharp

Write: "Move: [word]" — Keep it to ONE short description

4
The "Feel" Word

Say: "If you were standing inside that mark, would it feel more hard or more soft?"

Other contrasts: heavy/light, smooth/bumpy, warm/cold

Write: "Feel: [word]"

If they say "feels like a mountain," redirect: "That sounds like what the thing might be. For 'Feel,' let's pick just a body-word like hard, soft, rough, or smooth."

5
The Tiny "Helper" Word

Say: "Sometimes, when you made that fast mark, a tiny word kind of jumps into your head by itself. Not a whole story, just a small word like land, water, building, air, or nothing. Did a tiny word pop up this time?"

Options: land, water, building, air, none

If they hesitate: "If nothing jumps in quickly, we just write 'none' and that's okay."

Repeat the Sequence

After completing one run, say: "We're going to listen to the same number again and see what your hand does this time."

Repeat the entire sequence 2-4 times on the same target number, depending on attention span.

Giving Feedback

After completing your runs on a target number, show the child what that number was pointing to.

Choose Clear Targets

Use targets with strong overall character: open ocean, large mountain ranges, big cities, wide deserts, dense forests.

Point Out Connections

Your job is NOT to judge correct vs. incorrect. Point out meaningful connections.

If "Helper: water" and target is an ocean:

"This little word 'water' fits this place really well, doesn't it?"

If "Move: up" and target has a tall mountain:

"This 'up' fits the tall mountain here."

If a word doesn't match anything visible:

"This one we can't really match to anything in this picture."

Stay neutral. Don't say it's "wrong."

Understanding Breaks

Breaks are named ways to pause when the structure stops working. They teach the child that not forcing an answer is a valid and important response.

Miss Break

When to Use

  • Child says "I don't feel anything" right after the number
  • They stare at the page and can't make a mark
  • They slowly draw a picture instead of making a quick stroke

What to Say

"Let's call that a Miss. That just means we didn't catch the first quick thing this time. I'll write 'Miss' here on the side. Shake your hands out, look around the room, take a breath, and we'll try the same number again."

What to Do

Write "Miss" in the margin. Take a brief pause—shake hands, look around, breathe. Then repeat the number and start fresh.

Why This Works

It's not "you failed." It's "we didn't catch it that time." This keeps the experience light and allows them to try again without anxiety.

Story Break

When to Use

  • Child gives long, detailed stories instead of simple descriptors
  • Example: "It's a big red castle with dragons flying around and flags on top"

What to Say

"That's a story. Stories are fine, but they're different from the little clue words we're practicing. I'm going to write 'Story: big red castle' over here on the side so it has its own place. Then we'll go back to a new fast mark and our little words."

What to Do

Write "Story:" and a brief summary in the margin. Start fresh with the same number: fast mark, Move, Feel, Helper.

Why This Works

It validates stories as real experiences but clearly distinguishes them from descriptive data. The child learns different mental content has different places.

Confused Break

When to Use

  • Child says "It's all mixed up" or "I don't know what I'm doing"
  • You see clear frustration or signs of being overwhelmed

What to Say

"Let's take a Confused break. I'll write 'Confused' here. Let's stand up and stretch, maybe get a drink. Then we can decide if we want to try this number again or save it for another day."

What to Do

Write "Confused" in the margin. Actually stand up and move. After the reset, evaluate: try again, or end this session?

Why This Works

Confusion deserves acknowledgment. Sometimes the signal gets muddy, or they're tired. Naming it and taking a real break often clears things up.

Stage II: Quiet Senses

Stage II adds sensory awareness to the foundation you built in Stage I. The child learns to name simple bodily sensations they would experience at the target location—not what the place is, but what it would feel like to be there.

Critical: Stage II Builds On Stage I

Stage II does NOT replace Stage I. The child still does the complete Stage I sequence (number, fast mark, Move, Feel, Helper), and then adds the new "Senses" block immediately after.

Stage I must be solid before introducing Stage II.

What Stage II Adds

The Senses Block

After completing Stage I, the child adds 3-5 short sensory words: cold, windy, rough, quiet, bright.

This deepens sensory contact with the target without requiring analysis. They're noticing physical qualities, not naming the place.

Contrast Prompts

You guide through specific contrasts: warm or cold? smooth or rough? loud or quiet?

Binary choices prevent getting stuck and keep focus on immediate sensory impressions.

Shaping Away from Labels

When they say "train station," you park it as a story and extract the sensation: "So should we write 'loud' or 'quiet'?"

This teaches the crucial distinction between sensory data and analytical overlay.

The Complete Structure (Stage I + II)

For each target number, the work now appears in this order:

Secret number (e.g., 101)
Fast mark (squiggle)
Move: up
Feel: hard
Helper: land
Senses:
cold
windy
rough
bright

Stage I lines stay exactly the same. You simply add the Senses block underneath.

Running Stage II Sessions

Stage II sessions follow the same structure as Stage I, with the Senses block added after Helper. Sessions still take 20-25 minutes.

Introducing Stage II to the Child

Begin with a clear explanation before using secret numbers.

"Now that you've practiced the number, the fast squiggle, and your first little words, we're going to add a new skill."

"After we finish your Move, Feel, and Helper words, we'll ask: if you were standing at that place, what would your body feel?"

"Not 'what is it,' but things like: cold or warm, rough or smooth, loud or quiet, bright or dark. We'll write down three or four of those body-words."

Model with a Visible Example First

Before using secret numbers, show a simple photograph (beach, forest, city) and demonstrate:

You: "If we were standing here on this beach, what might your body feel?"

Child: "Hot... sandy... windy... loud waves"

You write:

Senses:
hot
sandy
windy
loud

You: "These are the kinds of words we'll add after your fast mark. We won't say 'beach' yet, only the body-words."

The Five Core Prompts

After Stage I is complete, write "Senses:" then lead through these questions. Stop when you have 3-5 sense words.

1. Temperature

"If you were standing at that place, would your body feel more warm or more cold?"

→ Write: "cold" or "warm"

2. Texture

"Does the ground or surface feel more smooth or more rough?"

→ Write: "rough" or "smooth"

3. Moisture

"Does it feel more wet or more dry there?"

→ Write: "wet" or "dry"

4. Sound

"Are your ears hearing more loud or more quiet?"

→ Write: "loud" or "quiet"

5. Light

"Is the place more bright or more dark?"

→ Write: "bright" or "dark"

You don't need all five prompts every time. Keep it light—3-5 words is plenty.

Shaping Away from Labels

Children will naturally offer place-names instead of sensations. Gently reshape this back to body-words.

Example: Story Instead of Sensation

You: "Are your ears hearing more loud or more quiet?"

Child: "It sounds like a train station with lots of people."

You respond: "That sounds like a story about what the place might be. Stories are okay, but they go in a different spot. For 'Senses' we only write body-words like loud or quiet."

Write in margin: Story: train station

Then ask: "So for 'Senses' here, should we write 'loud' or 'quiet'?"

Child: "Loud"

Write under Senses: loud

Example: Full Sentence Instead of Single Word

You: "Would your body feel more warm or more cold?"

Child: "Really cold and windy like on a mountain"

You respond: "That's good. For our list, we'll just write 'cold' right now. The 'windy' we can add next."

Write: cold

Then ask: "Does any wind show up—still or windy?"

Child: "Windy"

Write: windy

Stage II Feedback

When revealing the target, first point out Stage I fits, then shift to Senses.

If they wrote "cold" and picture shows snow:

"This sense word fits this picture really well. Can you see what makes it cold?"

If they wrote "windy" and picture shows waves with whitecaps:

"This 'windy' fits what we can see here—look at these waves."

If you can't tell from the picture:

"This one we can't really match or check from this picture."

You're still not scoring. You're showing that body-words sometimes connect directly with the hidden place.

Session Progression Over Time

Here's how to build skill session by session, from first introduction through mastery.

Stage I Progression (3-6 sessions)

First Session (15-20 min)

  • Explain the concept and demonstrate 1-2 fast marks yourself
  • Run two target numbers
  • For each number, guide through full sequence twice
  • Show pictures and point out clear connections

Second Session (20-25 min)

  • Use three target numbers
  • For each number, aim for 2-3 runs
  • Introduce Miss, Story, and Confused breaks when they occur
  • Let child write numbers and words if ready

Third+ Sessions (20-30 min)

  • Use 3-4 target numbers per session
  • Vary runs per target (2-4) based on engagement
  • Maintain consistent structure every time
  • Child manages most writing; you provide timing and corrections

Stage I is "In Place" When:

  • Child naturally expects: "Number → mark → Move → Feel → Helper"
  • Fast marks appear without hesitation
  • Move/Feel words come easily
  • They understand Helper vs. Story distinction
  • Breaks are used appropriately

Don't add Stage II until this pattern is solid and automatic.

Stage II Progression (3-5 sessions)

First Stage II Session (20-25 min)

  • Explain Stage II and model with visible example
  • Run two secret numbers
  • Guide through full Stage I + all 5 Senses prompts
  • Write sense words for them if needed
  • Point out Stage I fits, then Senses fits

Second Session (20-25 min)

  • Use 2-3 secret numbers
  • Continue contrast prompts but start asking without giving both options
  • If they stall or give labels, fall back to specific prompts
  • Use Story margin consistently

Third+ Sessions (20-30 min)

  • Use 3-4 target numbers
  • Prompt with open instruction: "Give me 3-4 body-words"
  • Only use specific contrasts if needed
  • Child manages most of process

Stage II is "In Place" When:

  • Child expects Senses after Helper without being told
  • Can give 2-3 body-words without every prompt
  • Accepts reshaping from labels without distress
  • Comfortable with all three breaks

Pattern is now stable: Number → Mark → Move → Feel → Helper → Senses

Key Teaching Tips

Keep Sessions Short

20-25 minutes is plenty. Better to end with energy left than push until exhausted.

Consistency Matters

Same header, same sequence, same language. The ritual helps the child relax into the structure.

Stay Neutral

Don't get excited at "hits" or disappointed at misses. Treat all responses as interesting data.

Model Occasionally

Show don't just tell. Demonstrate fast marks or sense words when needed.

Trust the Process

Some sessions feel better than others. The skill develops through repetition, not trying harder.

Celebrate Self-Correction

When they catch themselves starting a story and self-correct to data—that's real learning.
